A Formal Remonstrance
to the Houses of Congress

Concerning the Abandonment of Enumerated Powers and the Path to Autocracy

Anno Domini MMXXVI  ✦  Published by a Concerned Citizen  ✦  Richmond, Virginia

Gentlemen of the Senate and of the House of Representatives,

It is a circumstance which no man of sense can overlook, and which no sophistry of party spirit can long conceal, that the present disturbances convulsing our national councils are but the natural and inevitable fruit of a most long-standing, deliberate, and systematic abandonment of the boundaries set by our founding charter.

To observe those who sit in the seats of the Legislature — men who have for decades treated the enumerated powers of Article I as mere suggestions to be bypassed whenever the winds of political convenience blow in an unfavourable direction — now raising a clamour against the Executive branch, is a spectacle of hypocrisy so brazen and so thoroughly transparent, that it must provoke even the most dormant spirit to a state of absolute and righteous resentment.

I am writing this because my patience is quite exhausted. To witness the choreographed outrage, the practiced frowns, and the hollow, reverberating speeches concerning executive overreach is, to a thinking mind, genuinely nauseating. You are standing in the wreckage of a fire which you yourselves helped to kindle, acting as if you were the very persons attempting to preserve the building from a conflagration.

For decades, you have been the ones handing over the keys to our national house. You have unbolted the windows and dismantled the very locks that were designed to keep power in its proper place. And now that the door has been locked from the inside by a strength you encouraged, you have the nerve to act like victims of a sudden and unforeseen theft.

I.   The Nature of the Republic: Enumerated Powers & the Philosophy of Restraint

We were meant to be a Republic — a system built on fixed law and partitioned authority, not an unlimited democracy where the majority may act without restraint, nor a system where the Executive may govern by decree. Mr. Madison, writing in Federalist No. 45, made the condition of our Union perfectly clear: the powers delegated to the federal government were to be few and defined, while those remaining with the states were to be numerous and indefinite.

The Constitution is not an elastic document; it is a list. If a power is not found upon that list, you do not possess it. It is as simple, and as profound, as that. Yet, you have treated this sacred charter as if it were a common rag, tossing it aside whenever a policy was too difficult to pass through the proper channels or an election was too close to risk a difficult vote.

Every time you interpret a clause to mean its opposite, or hide behind the General Welfare to fund projects that the Founders would have found absurd, you are eroding the soil in which liberty grows. When you allow the Executive to interpret its own powers, or when you delegate your law-making authority to unelected boards and commissions, you are committing a slow suicide of the legislative branch.

II.   The Usurpation of the People's Voice & the Betrayal of Representation

Perhaps the most alarming of your derelictions is the manner in which you have hijacked the very concept of representation. You sit in your chambers and claim to speak with the voice of the people. Yet, it is a voice of your own manufacture — a synthetic clamour produced by political consultants. You have mistaken the echoes of your own rhetoric for the genuine will of the citizens you were elected to serve.

By substituting the actual needs and the quiet, steady liberty of the public for a series of choreographed political dramas, you have ceased to be representatives and have become a self-serving faction. You no longer represent the people; you merely use them as a rhetorical shield to protect your own institutional vanity.

"A representative who ignores the constitutional limits placed upon him by the people is no longer their servant, but their master in disguise."

III.   The Financial Subjugation of the States & the Proliferation of Fraud

We must now examine a corruption of the most extensive and debilitating nature — the systematic use of the federal treasury to purchase the sovereignty of the several States. Our Union was designed as a federation of independent republics, each a laboratory of liberty, each a check upon the central authority. Yet, through the insidious mechanism of grants-in-aid and federal subsidies, you have transformed these proud States into mere administrative departments of a national government.

You offer the people's own money back to their local governments, but only upon the condition that they submit to edicts you have no constitutional right to impose. Whether it be in the instruction of their youth, the policing of their streets, or the management of their very lands, the States have been taught to look to Washington with the eyes of a mendicant rather than the dignity of a sovereign.

This torrent of federal gold, disconnected as it is from the local accountability of the taxpayer, has birthed a culture of fraud that would shock the conscience of any honest man. In every State of this Union, we see funds intended for the relief of the suffering diverted to the pockets of political favorites. We see a vast and labyrinthine system of social programs where the administrative costs exceed the benefits provided, and where the lack of audit is not an accident but a design.

IV.   The Roman Parallel: The Senate That Built the Throne

History does not repeat, but it certainly rhymes — and you are currently reciting the final verses of the Roman Republic with a fidelity that is truly alarming. Rome's fall was not the work of a single day, nor the triumph of a single man. It was a slow, institutional rot that began in the very heart of its government: the Senate.
