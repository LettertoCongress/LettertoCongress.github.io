# A Formal Remonstrance to the Houses of Congress
### Concerning the Abandonment of Enumerated Powers and the Path to Autocracy

---

Gentlemen of the Senate and of the House of Representatives,

It is a circumstance which no man of sense can overlook, and which no sophistry of party spirit can long conceal, that the present disturbances convulsing our national councils are but the natural and inevitable fruit of a most long-standing, deliberate, and systematic abandonment of the boundaries set by our founding charter. To observe those who sit in the seats of the Legislature; men who have for decades treated the enumerated powers of Article I as mere suggestions to be bypassed whenever the winds of political convenience blow in an unfavourable direction; now raising a clamour against the Executive branch, is a spectacle of hypocrisy so brazen and so thoroughly transparent, that it must provoke even the most dormant spirit to a state of absolute and righteous resentment.

I am writing this because my patience is quite exhausted. To witness the choreographed outrage, the practiced frowns, and the hollow, reverberating speeches concerning executive overreach is, to a thinking mind, genuinely nauseating. You are standing in the wreckage of a fire which you yourselves helped to kindle, acting as if you were the very persons attempting to preserve the building from a conflagration. For decades, you have been the ones handing over the keys to our national house. You have unbolted the windows and dismantled the very locks that were were designed to keep power in its proper place. And now that the door has been locked from the inside by a strength you encouraged, you have the nerve to act like victims of a sudden and unforeseen theft.

---

## I. The Nature of the Republic: Enumerated Powers and the Philosophy of Restraint

We were meant to be a Republic; a system built on fixed law and partitioned authority, not an unlimited democracy where the majority may act without restraint, nor a system where the Executive may govern by decree. Mr. Madison, writing in *Federalist No. 45*, made the condition of our Union perfectly clear: the powers delegated to the federal government were to be few and defined, while those remaining with the states were to be numerous and indefinite. He did not offer this as a suggestion. He did not present it as a malleable theory to be discarded when the complexities of the age seemed too great. He offered it as a structural necessity to prevent the very tyranny we now face.

The Constitution is not an elastic document; it is a list. If a power is not found upon that list, you do not possess it. It is as simple, and as profound, as that. Yet, you have treated this sacred charter as if it were a common rag, tossing it aside whenever a policy was too difficult to pass through the proper channels or an election was too close to risk a difficult vote. Mr. Hamilton noted in *Federalist No. 78* that the legislature has no authority to pass acts contrary to the manifest tenor of the Constitution. By ignoring these boundaries, you have destroyed the very concept of limited government.

Every time you interpret a clause to mean its opposite, or hide behind the General Welfare to fund projects that the Founders would have found absurd, you are eroding the soil in which liberty grows. When you allow the Executive to interpret its own powers, or when you delegate your law-making authority to unelected boards and commissions, you are committing a slow suicide of the legislative branch. You have turned the supreme law of the land into a mere suggestion, and in doing so, you have invited the very autocracy you now pretend to fear.

---

## II. The Usurpation of the People's Voice and the Betrayal of Representation

Perhaps the most alarming of your derelictions is the manner in which you have hijacked the very concept of representation. You sit in your chambers and claim to speak with the voice of the people. Yet, it is a voice of your own manufacture; a synthetic clamour produced by political consultants. You have mistaken the echoes of your own rhetoric for the genuine will of the citizens you were elected to serve.

By substituting the actual needs and the quiet, steady liberty of the public for a series of choreographed political dramas, you have ceased to be representatives and have become a self-serving faction. You no longer represent the people; you merely use them as a rhetorical shield to protect your own institutional vanity. A representative who ignores the constitutional limits placed upon him by the people is no longer their servant, but their master in disguise. You have created a political class that is more loyal to its own survival than to the constituents it purports to defend.

---

## III. The Financial Subjugation of the States and the Proliferation of Fraud

We must now examine a corruption of the most extensive and debilitating nature; the systematic use of the federal treasury to purchase the sovereignty of the several States. Our Union was designed as a federation of independent republics, each a laboratory of liberty, each a check upon the central authority. Yet, through the insidious mechanism of grants-in-aid and federal subsidies, you have transformed these proud States into mere administrative departments of a national government.

You offer the people's own money back to their local governments, but only upon the condition that they submit to edicts you have no constitutional right to impose. Whether it be in the instruction of their youth, the policing of their streets, or the management of their very lands, the States have been taught to look to Washington with the eyes of a mendicant rather than the dignity of a sovereign. This is a betrayal of the federalist principle that is as profound as it is expensive.

Furthermore, this torrent of federal gold, disconnected as it is from the local accountability of the taxpayer, has birthed a culture of fraud that would shock the conscience of any honest man. When money is seen to fall from the heavens of the Capitol, it is treated by many as manna to be hoarded rather than a trust to be managed. We observe, with a mixture of horror and disgust, the billions of dollars that vanish into the maw of improper payments; a delicate term you employ to mask what is, in truth, a systemic theft from the public.

In every State of this Union, we see the evidence of your negligence. We see funds intended for the relief of the suffering diverted to the pockets of political favorites. We see infrastructure projects that serve only to enrich the contractors who fund your campaigns. We see a vast and labyrinthine system of social programs where the administrative costs exceed the benefits provided, and where the lack of audit is not an accident but a design. This fraud is the inevitable result of your desire to buy political favor with a currency you do not own. By bypassing the States' own constitutional limits, you have created a vacuum of oversight in which the dishonest and the predatory are permitted to feast. A government that cannot account for the money it takes by force from its citizens has forfeited its claim to their respect.

---

## IV. The Roman Parallel: The Senate That Built the Throne

History does not repeat, but it certainly rhymes; and you are currently reciting the final verses of the Roman Republic with a fidelity that is truly alarming. Rome's fall was not the work of a single day, nor the triumph of a single man. It was a slow, institutional rot that began in the very heart of its government; the Senate.

One must contrast the conduct of the early Roman Senate; which understood that their authority rested upon their character and their unyielding devotion to the *Mos Maiorum*; with the flaccidity of the later body. In 133 B.C., when the Gracchi brothers attempted to use the law to fix a broken system of land ownership, the Senate responded with violence. They taught the people that the law was negotiable and that force was the ultimate arbiter.

Then came Gaius Marius, who reformed the army so that soldiers were loyal to their generals instead of the state. The Senate sat by as the military became a political weapon, too afraid of the generals to assert the power of the law. By the time Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon, he was merely walking through a door that the Senate had left wide open for a hundred years. He did not have to overthrow a functioning Republic; he just had to step into the vacuum that your Roman predecessors had created through their own greed and indecision. You are the Senate that built the throne for Caesar. You have allowed the standards of the Republic to fall into the dust, and you now wonder why a man of ambition stoops to pick them up.

---

## V. The Weimar Parallel: How Democracies Hollow Themselves

Look at the more recent tragedy of the Weimar Republic. It was dismantled from within by its own leaders; men who thought they were being practical and modern. The Reichstag became so paralyzed by factional fighting that they started relying on Article 48; the emergency power clause; to get anything done.

They thought they could use the Executive to bypass the difficult work of building a legislative consensus. By 1933, the legislature had basically voted itself into irrelevance. The Enabling Act was but the final rubber stamp on a surrender that had been happening for a decade. When you pass trillion-dollar bills that no one has read, or when you fund wars through resolutions instead of declarations, you are walking the Weimar path. You are hollowing out the institution of Congress until it is nothing but a hollow shell, waiting for someone with enough ambition to crush it. Power once surrendered is never returned. It is merely concentrated elsewhere, usually in the hands of those who have no intention of giving it back.

---

## VI. The Catalogue of Derelictions: A Century of Betrayal

Your failure is not a theory; it is a matter of documented record. Your dereliction of duty has been systematic and sustained across generations.

**The War Power and the Abandonment of Article I:** In 1950, you permitted President Truman to send Americans to their deaths in Korea under the label of a "police action." There was no declaration of war. You bypassed Article I, Section 8 because it was easier than having a debate. This established a precedent that the President could commit the lives of citizens without the express consent of their representatives. Since that day, the war power of Congress has become a ghost. You have handed the most terrible power of a state; the power to kill and be killed; to a single individual, and then you wonder why the Executive feels emboldened to ignore you on lesser matters.

**The Administrative State and the Bureaucratic Coup:** You have allowed a massive, unelected fourth branch of government to take over. This is an injury to the very soul of the citizen. The law is no longer a known and fixed rule, but a shifting set of edicts issued by swarms of Officers who are accountable to no one. You have outsourced your job to bureaucrats because you were too busy with the theater of politics to do the work of governing. You have created a system where the citizen is governed by the whim of an agency rather than the law of his representative. This is a betrayal of the very concept of consent of the governed.

**The Appropriations Power and the Loss of Sovereignty:** You have turned the power of the purse into a joke. Instead of budgets, we get continuing resolutions. Madison said the power of the purse is "the most complete and effectual weapon of the people's representatives." You have dropped that weapon. A body that cannot pass a budget is not a government; it is a committee of supplicants waiting for the Executive to tell them what they may spend. By refusing to engage in the difficult work of appropriation, you have surrendered the primary mechanism of legislative control.

---

## VIII. The Moral Obligation of the Individual Representative

It is a principle which every man of honor must hold dear, that a body of five hundred and thirty-five individuals cannot be held accountable unless each man is first held accountable for his own conduct. We must speak of the moral obligation that rests upon your shoulders; not as a collective, but as individuals who have taken a solemn oath before the Almighty. Every time you cast a vote for a bill of several thousand pages which you have not read, or every time you remain silent in the face of an act that you know to be outside the bonds of the law because it benefits your faction, you are personally contributing to the dissolution of the Republic.

There is a particular kind of cowardice that infects the halls of the Capitol; a belief that one's own small compromise is of no consequence in the grand scale of the nation. But a thousand such compromises constitute a revolution. You have a moral duty to the citizens who sent you here to be their voice, not their echo. When you permit the Executive to act outside his bonds, you are telling the people that their votes no longer matter, for the man they elected to represent them has chosen to be a mere spectator to his own irrelevance.

If a man takes an oath to support and defend the Constitution, and then proceeds to treat that document as a list of inconveniences to be managed, what can we say of his character? He is a man who has forfeited his honor for the sake of a title. You may retain your offices and your pensions, but you have lost the right to the respect of a free people. A representative who fears his party leadership more than he fears the judgment of history is a man who has already failed his country.

---

## IX. The Corruption of the Federal-State Pipeline and the Spectacle of Fraud

We must return to the matter of the federal treasury, for it is the source of the most pervasive rot in our system. You have used the people's wealth to create a system of dependency that has hollowed out the sovereignty of the States. By offering grants with strings attached, you have forced the several States to adopt policies that their own citizens would never have approved. This is a form of financial despotism that masquerades as benevolence.

Furthermore, the lack of oversight in these distributions has invited a degree of fraud that would be considered criminal in any private enterprise. We see States receiving hundreds of millions of dollars for projects that exist only in the imaginations of the bureaucrats who propose them. We see emergency funds diverted to satisfy the whims of local political machines. Because the money is seen as "federal"; which is to say, belonging to no one; it is spent with a recklessness that borders on the insane.

The fraud we observe is not merely a matter of missing dollars; it is a matter of missing character. You have created a system that rewards the dishonest and penalizes the prudent. A State that manages its finances with wisdom is rewarded with less federal aid, while a State that squanders its resources is bailed out with the taxes of its neighbors. This is a perversion of justice that undermines the very social contract. You have made yourselves the masters of a vast machine of patronage, and in doing so, you have made the citizen a mere subject of your financial caprice.

---

## X. The Path of Restoration, Pillar I: Reclaiming the War Power

If you wish to save this Republic, you must begin by reclaiming the most solemn of your duties; the power to declare war. It is frequently urged by the timid that the speed of modern weaponry and the complexity of global alliances leave no time for the deliberations of a legislature. This is a fallacy of the most dangerous kind. The speed of a missile does not change the nature of a Republic.

If a cause is so urgent that it requires the blood of our citizens, it is urgent enough to demand the constitutional consent of their representatives. To allow a single man to send the nation to war is to return to the very monarchical whims our fathers fought to escape. You must pass a resolution immediately stating that no funds shall be expended for any military action that has not been formally declared by Congress, with a clearly defined enemy and an explicit date of expiration. The excuse that diplomacy requires flexibility is but a cover for *power requires secrecy*. You must choose between the convenience of the Executive and the liberty of the citizen.

---

## XI. The Path of Restoration, Pillar II: Ending the Bureaucratic Coup

You must pass the REINS Act or its equivalent without delay. It is argued by your friends in the administrative agencies that the technical nature of modern life; the intricacies of environmental regulation, the complexities of financial markets; requires experts to write the rules. But a rule that carries the weight of law and the threat of punishment is a law, and the Constitution grants the law-making power solely to you.

If a regulation is too technical for you to understand, then it has no place in a free society. To delegate this power is to admit that you are either too incompetent to govern or too lazy to try. Every major regulation that imposes a significant cost upon the people or their businesses must be voted upon by you. You must take the responsibility for the laws you impose. The people did not elect experts to rule them; they elected you to represent them. To do otherwise is to concede that we are no longer a Republic, but a technocracy governed by an unelected elite.

---

## XII. The Path of Restoration, Pillar III: Enforcing the Appropriations Power

We must now speak of "the most complete and effectual weapon," as Mr. Madison termed it, with which a constitution can arm the representatives of the people; the power of the purse. You have, through a mixture of partisan cowardice and institutional sloth, allowed this weapon to rust in its scabbard. It is argued by those who prefer the ease of the present system that the complexities of a modern state, with its trillions in obligations and its vast array of departments, make a traditional budget impossible. They suggest that the continuing resolution is a necessary tool for the stability of the nation.

I reply that this is a falsehood designed to shield you from the consequences of your own indecision. A continuing resolution is not a budget; it is a confession of failure. It is a shroud under which you hide the most shameful of fiscal excesses and the most egregious of frauds. If you cannot agree upon the expenditures of the people's money within the time allotted by law, then you have no right to touch a single penny of it.

I propose a most rigorous remedy. If a full and transparent budget is not passed by the first day of the fiscal year, then all funding for the Legislature itself; including your salaries, your staffs, and your very travel; must cease immediately. Furthermore, the Executive must be forbidden from shifting a single dollar between accounts without an explicit vote of your body. You have allowed the power of the purse to become a power of the trough, where the States and the well-connected feast upon the labor of the citizen while you look the other way to avoid a difficult vote. You must reclaim the sovereignty of the budget, or you must admit that you are no longer a sovereign branch of government.

---

## XIII. The Path of Restoration, Pillar IV: Fulfilling the Confirmation Obligation

There is a most unseemly habit that has taken root in the Executive branch, and which you have permitted to grow unchecked; the use of acting officials to fill the highest offices of the state. It is argued that the broken nature of the confirmation process; a process which you, through your own partisan vitriol, have broken; necessitates these temporary measures to ensure the continuity of government.

I say that this is an evasion of the most dangerous kind. The Constitution requires that you provide Advice and Consent for the principal officers of the government. To allow a man to exercise the powers of a Cabinet Secretary or a director of an agency without your formal approval is to allow a shadow government to operate outside the reach of the law. It permits the President to appoint those whom he knows you would never confirm, thereby bypassing the primary check upon his personal influence.

You must establish a rule, as unyielding as the law of the Medes and Persians, that any nominee not confirmed or rejected within ninety days shall be deemed rejected, and the position must remain vacant and unfunded until a new name is presented and approved. Furthermore, no individual may serve in an acting capacity for more than thirty days. If you are afraid to vote upon a nominee because it might prove politically difficult, then you are afraid to perform the duty of your office. A Senate that will not vote is a Senate that has ceased to exist.

---

## XIV. The Restoration of Local Sovereignty and the End of Federal Bribes

To truly address the fraud we have discussed, you must go further than mere audits. You must dismantle the very system of federal grants that has made the States into your dependents. It will be said that the States need this money for their schools, their roads, and their poor. I reply that it is the people's money to begin with. If the people of a State wish to fund a project, let them do so through their own legislatures, where they can see the money spent and hold their own officials to account.

By taking money from the citizen, sending it to Washington to be diminished by a vast bureaucracy, and then granting it back to the States with a thousand conditions, you have created a system that is perfectly designed for corruption. It is in this labyrinth of federal requirements and state matching funds that the billion-dollar frauds we see today find their hiding places. You must return the taxing power and the governing power to the States. Stop the bribes, stop the conditions, and you will stop the fraud.

---

## XV. The Closing: The Final Judgment of the Citizenry

I have written these thousands of words not because I believe you will find them pleasant, but because I believe they are true. I have watched for years as you have paved the road for an autocracy you now pretend to fear. I have seen you trade the birthright of a free people for the pottage of your own re-election.

The question that remains is whether there is yet enough virtue in your body to turn back. Restoration will not come from another hollow speech or a partisan investigation. It will only come through a return to the fixed and certain boundaries of the Constitution. You must decide whether you are the guardians of a Republic or the managers of a decay.

I do not expect you to change because of the elegance of my prose; I expect you to change only if you realize that the patience of the American people is at its end. We are a people who have long suffered, but we are also a people who remember that our government is our servant, not our master.

Until you act with the courage and the honor that your station requires; until you act once more as the true representatives of a free people; I shall continue to hold you in the contempt you have earned.
